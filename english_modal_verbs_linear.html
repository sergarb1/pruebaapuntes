Part 1: The Foundations

Understanding what makes Modal Verbs unique.

Defining Modals

Modals don't describe what happens, but how the speaker feels about it.

Subjective Mood

They express possibility, obligation, permission, or ability.

Action vs. Attitude

"I walk" (Fact) vs. "I should walk" (Advice/Attitude).

The 4 Golden Rules

1. No -S for He/She

She musts → She must
He cans → He can

2. No "TO" after

I must to study → I must study
(Except 'ought to')

3. Inversion for Questions

Do you can? → Can you?
Does he must? → Must he?

4. No Continuous/Perfect

I am musting ❌
I have could ❌

Part 2: Ability & Possibility

Can, Could, and Be able to.

Present Ability: CAN

Use CAN for general skills and abilities in the present.

- I can speak English fluently.
- My sister can play the piano.
- Can you swim 100 meters?
- They can't hear you.
Note: We also use CAN for informal requests.

Past Ability: COULD

Use COULD for general skills in the past.

- I could run fast when I was 10.
- My grandfather could speak 5 languages.
- Could you walk when you were one?
- We couldn't find the keys.

The "Specific Success"

For a specific occasion in the past where you succeeded, use "was/were able to" instead of could.

General (Could)

"I could swim." (I had the skill in my childhood).

Specific (Able to)

"The car broke down, but I was able to fix it." (One-time success).

Quick Check: Ability

Which is correct?

A. I could win the race yesterday although I was tired.
B. I was able to win the race yesterday although I was tired. ✅
Explanation: Winning a specific race is a one-time success, so "was able to" is better than "could".

Part 3: Social English

Permission, Requests, and Suggestions.

The Permission Scale

Can I?
Informal
(Friends)

Could I?
Polite
(Strangers)

May I?
Formal
(Authority)

- "Can I use your charger, mate?"
- "Could I borrow your umbrella for a second?"
- "May I see your passport, please?"

Very Polite Requests

Would you mind...? is the ultimate weapon of politeness.

- "Would you mind closing the window?"
- "Would you mind helping me with these bags?"
Grammar Alert: Always use -ING after "Would you mind".

Shall I / We...?

Use SHALL for offers and suggestions (only with I and We).

Offers

"Shall I carry your suitcase?"

Suggestions

"Shall we dance?"

Part 4: Advice & Obligation

Should, Must, Have to, and Need to.

Giving Advice

Should

Opinion / Recommendation.

"You should study more."

Ought to

Moral duty / Formal.

"We ought to help the poor."

Had Better

Strong warning.

"You had better stop that!"
- "You should wear a coat." (Suggestion)
- "You had better wear a coat, or you'll get sick." (Warning)

Obligation Scenarios

Must (Internal)

Personal decision / Authority.

"I must stop smoking." (I feel it's right).

Have to (External)

Rules / Laws / Jobs.

"I have to wear a tie." (The company rules).
Tip: In everyday English, people often use "have to" for both.

The Danger Zone

Mustn't

Forbidden. Prohibition.

- You mustn't park here.
- You mustn't tell anyone!

Don't have to

Optional. No necessity.

- You don't have to pay now.
- You don't have to come.
CRITICAL: "Mustn't" means NO. "Don't have to" means MAYBE.

Part 5: Logic & Deduction

Must, Can't, and Might.

Logical Deduction

"He has 10 cars. He must be rich."
"The phone is ringing. It might be John."
"It's 40°C. It can't be snowing."

Quick Check: Deduction

"Look at those dark clouds. It ________ rain."

A. must
B. might ✅
C. can't
Explanation: Clouds make it possible/likely, but not 100% certain.

Part 6: Expert Modals

Past Modals and Nuances.

The Past Modals

Structure: Modal + Have + Past Participle (V3)

Should have + V3

Regret or past criticism.

"I should have studied!"

Must have + V3

Logic about the past.

"He must have forgotten."

The Past Modals (Cont.)

Could have + V3

Opportunity not taken.

"I could have won the race."

Needn't have + V3

Useless action done in past.

"You needn't have cooked."
Needn't have means you DID it, but it was a waste of time.

Used To vs. Would

Category Used to Would
Repeated Actions ✅ YES ✅ YES
States (Be, Live, Have) ✅ YES ❌ NO
"I used to live in Paris." (OK)
"I would live in Paris." (WRONG)
