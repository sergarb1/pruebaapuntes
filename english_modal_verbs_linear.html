What are Modal Verbs?

Modal verbs are "helper" verbs that express modality. They don't describe a physical action, but the attitude or condition of that action.

The 4 Golden Rules

Immutable Forms

They never change. No -s, -ed, or -ing.

He cans → He can

The Bare Infinitive

No "to" (except ought to).

I must to study → I must study

Direct Questions

No 'do' or 'does'. Just swap.

Do you can? → Can you?

Direct Negatives

Just add 'not' after the modal.

I cannot (can't) go.

Ability & Possibility

General

Can

"I can swim."
Past

Could

"I could run fast."
Specific

Be able to

"I will be able to fix it."
Key Nuance: Use "was able to" for specific past achievements. "I was able to find the keys" (not could).

Advice & Suggestions

Should

Opinion / General recommendation.

"You should study."

Ought to

Moral duty / Formal advice.

"We ought to help."

Had Better

Strong warning with consequences.

"You had better stop!"

Obligation

Must (Internal)

The speaker feels it's necessary.

"I must call my mom."

Have to (External)

Rules, laws, or circumstances.

"I have to wear a uniform."

Prohibition vs Optional

Mustn't

Prohibition

It's forbidden. Illegal. Don't do it!

"You mustn't smoke here."

Don't have to

Lack of Necessity

It's not necessary. You can if you want.

"You don't have to pay now."

Logical Deduction

"He has 5 cars. He must be rich."
"The phone is ringing. It might be John."
"It's 40°C. It can't be snowing."

Social English

  • Can I...? (Informal)
  • Could I...? (Polite)
  • May I...? (Formal)
  • Would you mind...? (Very Polite)
  • Shall we...? (Offer/Suggestion)

Past Modals

Structure: Modal + Have + Past Participle (V3)

Should have + V3

Regret or past criticism.

"I should have studied!"

Must have + V3

Logic about the past.

"He must have forgotten."

Could have + V3

Past opportunity not used.

"I could have won."

Needn't have + V3

Useless action done in the past.

"You needn't have cleaned."

Past Habits

Feature Used To Would
Repeated Actions YES YES
Past States (Life, Be, Live) YES NO
"I used to live in Paris." (Correct)
"I would live in Paris." (Incorrect)

The "British" Modals

Shall

Used for offers and suggestions (mainly with I/We).

"Shall I open the window?"

Needn't

The formal way to say 'don't need to'.

"You needn't worry about the cost."
